# Working Notes

## Core theoretical claim

Morphosyntactic countability is a **homeostatic property cluster** (HPC), not a feature bundle or prototype category. The clustering is maintained by a specific mechanism: **bidirectional inference** between form and meaning.

- **Form → Meaning**: Count syntax triggers inference of individuated construal
- **Meaning → Form**: Individuated construal triggers selection of count syntax

This is genuinely homeostatic (not just clustered) because perturbations create pressure toward restoration—though "weak homeostasis" allows stable intermediate states when functional pressure anchors them.

---

## Three levels of "countability" (terminological hygiene)

| Level | Term | Domain | Example |
|-------|------|--------|---------|
| Ontological | **Discreteness** | World structure | Rice grains are discrete but construed as mass |
| Semantic | **Individuation** | Linguistic construal | *furniture* has individuated referents, mass syntax |
| Morphosyntactic | **Countability** | Grammar | The cluster of properties this paper addresses |

Most confusion in the literature comes from conflating these.

## The dissociation hierarchy

Properties sorted by linkage tightness (how precisely they require individuation):

**Tight** (lost first when individuation degrades):
- Singular form
- *a(n)* compatibility
- Low cardinal compatibility (*three N*)

**Moderate**:
- *several N*
- Distributive quantifiers (*each*, *every*)

**Loose** (retained longest):
- *many/few N*
- High round cardinals (*a hundred N*)
- Plural agreement

**Prediction**: Properties lost in order tight → loose. No noun should show the reverse pattern.

## Why this is HPC, not just prototype

Prototype theory predicts gradience but not **ordered** gradience. It says *cattle* is a "worse" count noun than *dog* but doesn't predict which properties are lost first.

The HPC account predicts:
1. Implicational structure (tight lost before loose)
2. Resistance to dissociation (intermediate states unstable unless functionally anchored)
3. Cross-linguistic recurrence (same hierarchy in other languages)
4. Processing signatures (mismatches costly, scaled by degree)

---

## The stability problem: why hasn't *police* drifted?

If the mechanism creates pressure toward cluster completion/dissolution, why are some intermediates stable?

**Answer**: Functional anchoring. *Police* has *officer* as a singulative alternative. Speakers who need to refer to individual police use *officer*, so there's no pressure to extend *a police* or *three police*. The quasi-count state is stable because the functional niche is served.

*Folks* lacks this escape valve (no *\*folker*), which explains why it's under more pressure and may be drifting.

## Probe design

**2 × 2 × 2 design**:
- Determinative: tight (*three*) vs. moderate (*several*)
- Construction: existential vs. agentive
- Noun: *folks* vs. *people* (control)

| | Existential | Agentive |
|---|---|---|
| **three people** | There are three people in the room. | Three people pushed the car. |
| **three folks** | There are three folks in the room. | Three folks pushed the car. |
| **several people** | There are several people in the room. | Several people pushed the car. |
| **several folks** | There are several folks in the room. | Several folks pushed the car. |

**Prediction**: *three folks* shows larger existential–agentive difference than *three people*. Existentials foreground "how many discrete entities" (high individuation demand); agentives allow aggregate construal.

Add *cattle* items as additional comparison:
- There are three cattle in the field. (predicted: low)
- Three cattle escaped from the field. (predicted: still low, but maybe slightly better?)

---

## Falsifiability

The account is disconfirmed if:

1. **Reverse implication**: A noun accepts *three N* but rejects *many N*
2. **The a–many split**: A noun accepts *a(n) N* but requires *much N* (not *many N*)
3. No intermediate cases show drift in either direction over time

---

## Connections to other frameworks

**Compatible with predictive processing**: Low cardinals generate high-precision predictions about atomic boundaries; *many* generates low-precision predictions. Tight-linkage properties = high precision = larger prediction error when violated. But don't bet the paper on this—mention as "compatible interpretation."

**Compatible with CGEL descriptivism**: This systematizes CGEL's "quasi-count" category without requiring generative apparatus.

**Contrasts with Borer**: Borer's DivP account explains that count/mass syntax exists but not why lexeme-specific preferences cluster. The HPC account can layer under syntactic approaches.

### Jong-Bok Kim (Dec 2025) - SBCG perspective

Kim responded to questions about modeling deitality/definiteness as HPCs in SBCG/HPSG. One point directly relevant to countability:

> "A tricky part would be that these features would be relevant only on phrasal level expressions, determined by context. For instance, 'He is an Einstein..', the proper noun is converted as a countable one."

**Relevance:** This supports the view that countability is phrasal/constructional rather than purely lexical. The "conversion" Kim notes is exactly the kind of bidirectional inference the HPC mechanism predicts - context (NP-level construction) shifts the countability properties.

**Full correspondence:** See `HPC book/correspondence/kim-jongbok-2025-12.md`

### Related: Transparent Free Relatives (`Transparent_free_relatives/`)

Kim proposed collaboration on TFRs (Dec 27). TFR transparency is another example of **phrasal feature percolation**: internal predicate properties (countability, definiteness) become visible at the phrase level. The mechanism is similar to what happens with countability in "He is an Einstein" - the construction level determines the features, not just the lexical item.

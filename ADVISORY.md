## I. STEPHEN TOULMIN — Argumentation

**Primary recommendation: Make the inferential structure visible.**

The paper's central argument has the form:

- **Data:** Count properties cluster; when they dissociate, they do so in order.
- **Claim:** Morphosyntactic countability is an HPC maintained by bidirectional inference.
- **Warrant:** Bidirectional inference links all count properties to a common semantic variable (individuation), making the cluster self-reinforcing and generating ordered dissociation when that variable weakens.

The *data* are well-presented. The *claim* is clear. The *warrant* is asserted but not independently established. This is the paper's argumentative vulnerability.

**Specific actions:**

1. **Foreground acquisition as the primary warrant.** The claim that children treat count properties as evidence for individuation—and overgeneralize accordingly—is your strongest empirical support for the mechanism. Currently it's one paragraph in §3.2. Promote it. If Barner (2005) or other acquisition studies show children extending *three* to nouns where they've only heard *many*, say so prominently. That's evidence for *inference*, not just correlation.

2. **Distinguish the descriptive claim from the mechanistic claim.** The hierarchy (tight → loose) is a descriptive generalization that could survive even if the mechanism turns out to be wrong. The bidirectional-inference story is an explanation of that generalization. Readers should know which is which. Consider a sentence like: "The implicational hierarchy is the empirical finding; bidirectional inference is the proposed explanation. The hierarchy could stand even if the mechanism is revised."

3. **Qualify the mechanism as inference to the best explanation.** You don't have direct evidence that speakers are performing bidirectional inference in real time. You have a pattern consistent with such inference. That's legitimate but should be flagged. "The pattern is what we'd expect if bidirectional inference were operating; direct psycholinguistic evidence would strengthen the case."

## II. KENNETH BURKE — Rhetorical Ontology

**Primary recommendation: Re-dramatize the mechanism.**

The paper reifies "the mechanism" as something that "operates," "maintains," "produces." This is convenient but obscures agency. Who is doing what to whom?

**Specific actions:**

1. **Name the agents.** Replace "the mechanism operates in acquisition, processing, and production" with something like: "Learners acquiring the system, speakers producing utterances, and hearers interpreting them all participate in the same inferential practice—and that shared practice is what holds the cluster together."

2. **Use representative anecdotes, not just examples.** The *pease → pea* case is currently a datum. Make it a story. "Speakers of Middle English heard *pease* with a final /z/ and, interpreting it as plural, back-formed a singular *pea*. The count cluster didn't just emerge; speakers *built* it, one inference at a time." That's three sentences and transforms an example into a narrative.

3. **Acknowledge the terministic screen.** You're committed to "countability," "individuation," "precision"—terms that highlight certain features and hide others. A single sentence of reflexivity would inoculate you: "The terminology I use foregrounds the cognitive-semantic dimension; an equally valid analysis might foreground social or interactional dimensions, which I largely set aside."

## III. JACQUELINE JONES ROYSTER — Ethos & Positionality

**Primary recommendation: Situate the data explicitly.**

The paper theorizes "English countability" but draws on a narrow empirical base: CGEL, which documents standard British and American educated usage. This isn't a flaw—all data come from somewhere—but it should be acknowledged.

**Specific actions:**

1. **Add a scoping statement early.** In §1 or §2, one sentence: "The data here are drawn from descriptive grammars of contemporary standard English (primarily CGEL); the hierarchy's applicability to other varieties, registers, and historical stages is an empirical question the framework generates but does not resolve."

2. **Reframe register variation as evidence, not noise.** You note that agricultural speakers accept *three cattle* "in specialized registers." That's not a marginal case to dismiss; it's evidence for the precision-demand story. If communicative context raises the need for exact enumeration, even quasi-count nouns can be pressed into service. Say so.

3. **Acknowledge the social mechanisms of maintenance.** The stability of the count/mass distinction in standard English is partly maintained by editors, teachers, style guides, and automated grammar checkers. These aren't external to the homeostatic system; they *are* part of it. A sentence in §3.2 or §7.3: "For standardized varieties, institutional norm-enforcement (style guides, editorial practice, language instruction) constitutes an additional homeostatic mechanism, maintaining the cluster at the community level as well as in individual grammars."

## IV. ANDREA LUNSFORD — Prose & Pedagogy

**Primary recommendation: Let one noun travel through the whole paper.**

The argument is well-structured but abstract. Readers track a parade of nouns—*rice, sand, furniture, cattle, police, folks, scissors, data*—without a stable anchor.

**Specific actions:**

1. **Choose a "home base" noun and return to it.** I'd suggest *cattle*. Introduce it in §2 as the paradigm quasi-count case. Return to it in §3 when explaining the mechanism. Show its place in the hierarchy in §4. Contrast it with *folks* in §5. Use it as the stable point against which other cases are measured.

2. **Add a visual scaffold.** The noun × property table is useful but doesn't show the triangular structure. Consider a figure—a simple diagram with *book* at one corner (full count), *furniture* at another (full mass), and *cattle/police/folks* along the diagonal between them. Visual learners will grasp the hierarchy faster from a figure than from prose.

3. **Make the signposting more explicit.** The paper has good forward references ("the hierarchy appears in §4"), but could use more backward references ("As noted in §2, individuation is a construal operation; we now see how that construal drives the dissociation"). This helps readers who've lost the thread.

4. **Consider a "For instructors" footnote or appendix.** Given your EAP background, a note on how this framework might inform teaching countability to L2 learners would extend the paper's impact without cluttering the main argument.

## V. CHIMAMANDA NGOZI ADICHIE — Narrative & Illustration

**Primary recommendation: Give the abstraction human stakes.**

The paper explains *what* happens to countability clusters and *how* the mechanism works. It doesn't show *why anyone should care* outside the seminar room.

**Specific actions:**

1. **Open with a puzzle that hooks.** The current opening is accurate but dry: "Count properties in English hang together strikingly." What if you started with a learner error or a copy-editor's dilemma? "A student writes *I bought three furnitures*; a copy editor debates *this data is* versus *these data are*; a Southerner says *you folks* where a Northerner says *you guys*. What do these have in common? They're all negotiations at the boundary of English countability—a boundary that turns out to be more structured than anyone realized."

2. **Humanize the *folks* case.** You say *folks* packages referents as "in-group collectives with a strong solidarity flavour." That's social! That's about belonging! Let the reader feel it. "When a Texan says *folks around here*, she's not just referring to people; she's claiming them as her own. The word's warmth is inseparable from its grammar—and that grammar is unstable in ways the word's warmth obscures."

3. **End with stakes, not just summary.** The conclusion currently summarizes and suggests extensions. What if the final paragraph gestured at broader implications? "If grammatical categories are homeostatic clusters rather than monolithic features, then the borders of grammar are not walls but gradients—maintained, negotiated, and occasionally redrawn by every speaker who uses them."

# ADDENDUM A: Geoffrey K. Pullum

**On the descriptive foundation**

The paper's use of CGEL's quasi-count category is accurate, but the category itself is a descriptive convenience, not a theoretical primitive. The nouns grouped under "quasi-count" may have arrived at their current profiles by different routes: *cattle* and *police* have been plural-only for centuries; *clergy* is arguably a collective rather than a true quasi-count; *vermin* shows mass/count alternation across registers. Treating them as a unified class for purposes of the HPC argument is legitimate—they do share distributional properties—but the paper should not assume that shared distribution entails shared underlying structure.

**On stress-testing the hierarchy**

The empirical base is narrow: seven nouns in the main table, a handful more discussed in prose. Before publication, I'd recommend a systematic survey of:

- Singular-plural syncretisms: *sheep, fish, deer, aircraft, species, offspring*
- Collective nouns with plural agreement (BrE): *team, committee, government, family*
- Recent loanwords and neologisms: how do new nouns get assigned countability?
- Corpus-derived frequency data: are the acceptability judgments stable across speakers?

If the hierarchy holds across these cases, it's robust. If exceptions emerge, they'll sharpen the account.

**On semantic precision**

The paper's claim that "precision" of individuation explains the ordering is intuitively plausible but underspecified. What, exactly, clashes when *three* combines with *cattle*? If *cattle* denotes a set of bovines, why can't *three* count elements of that set? The answer presumably involves something like: *cattle* denotes a collective entity without accessible atomic structure, so cardinal quantifiers fail. But then *many cattle* should fail too, since *many* also quantifies over individuals.

The paper's answer—*many* tolerates "vague" atoms—is a theoretical posit that needs engagement with formal semantics. Grimm's (2018) work on aggregates, Rothstein's (2010) atomicity conditions, and Chierchia's (1998) mass/count parameters are all relevant. The paper cites them but doesn't show how its "precision" story maps onto their formalisms. A footnote or paragraph doing that mapping would strengthen the theoretical grounding.

**On the broader claim**

Despite these caveats, the paper makes a genuine contribution. The implicational hierarchy—tight properties lost before loose—is a real generalization that CGEL documents but does not explain. The HPC framing is novel in this domain. If the mechanism holds up, it would unify a range of facts that currently look arbitrary.

My recommendation: *publish with revisions*. Stress-test the empirical base, engage more deeply with formal semantics, and be explicit about what's established versus what's hypothesized. The argument is strong enough to survive scrutiny if you don't overclaim.

# ADDENDUM B: Mohammad-Ali Khalidi

**On the HPC framework**

The application of HPC to grammatical categories is novel and, in my view, legitimate—provided the key components are in place:

1. **A cluster of properties** that reliably co-occur. ✓ The paper establishes this.
2. **Homeostatic mechanisms** that maintain the cluster over time. ⚠️ Partially developed.
3. **Tolerance for variation**: members can lack some properties; borderline cases exist. ✓ The quasi-count nouns are precisely such cases.
4. **Projectibility**: if something belongs to the kind, you can make reliable predictions about its other properties. ✓ If a noun accepts *many*, you predict it accepts plural agreement.

The weak point is (2). The paper asserts that bidirectional inference maintains the cluster but doesn't show this empirically. The acquisition literature (Barner et al.) provides indirect support; foregrounding it would help. But the deeper issue is the *level* at which homeostasis operates.

**On levels of homeostasis**

Boyd's original HPC framework was designed for biological species, where the homeostatic mechanisms (genetic transmission, development) operate at the individual-organism level and propagate through reproduction at the population level. The two levels are integrated.

In language, the analogous levels are:

- **Individual grammar**: Each speaker's mental representation of countability. Maintained by acquisition, processing, production.
- **Community norm**: The shared conventions that make communication possible. Maintained by interaction, correction, standardization.

The paper focuses almost entirely on the individual level. But grammatical categories are *social* kinds as well as cognitive ones. The stability of standard English countability depends on institutions—schools, style guides, dictionaries—that don't exist for vernacular varieties or emergent dialects. If you're claiming that countability is an HPC *in English*, you need to specify whose English and acknowledge that the homeostatic regime may differ across communities.

**On extending the framework**

I'd encourage the author to see this paper as opening a research programme rather than closing a question. The HPC framework, applied to grammar, raises further questions:

- Do all grammatical categories have HPC structure, or only some?
- What distinguishes categories with strong homeostasis (e.g., noun/verb) from those with weak homeostasis (e.g., count/mass boundary)?
- Can we compare homeostatic "tightness" across languages?

The concluding gesture toward transitivity, finiteness, and the noun/verb distinction is suggestive but undeveloped. A future paper could explore one of these cases in comparable depth.

**On the relationship to natural kinds**

The paper doesn't take a stand on whether morphosyntactic countability is a *natural kind* in the philosophical sense. It doesn't need to. The HPC framework was developed for natural kinds but doesn't require that every HPC be natural in the strong sense (mind-independent, supporting laws of nature). Social and psychological kinds can have HPC structure too, provided the mechanisms are real.

What the paper *does* need to establish is that the mechanisms are doing genuine explanatory work—that "bidirectional inference" isn't just a redescription of the correlation between count syntax and individuation. The acquisition overgeneralization data are the best evidence for this. If children infer *three N* from *many N*, that's evidence of a *productive* mechanism, not just a statistical regularity.

**Final assessment**

The paper is doing something important: demonstrating that a grammatical category can be fruitfully analyzed as an HPC. If the argument holds, it opens new ways of thinking about category structure in linguistics. The framework is appropriate; the application is careful; the residual issue is empirical depth.

My recommendation: *accept with revisions* addressing the level-of-homeostasis issue and foregrounding the acquisition evidence. The paper is already competitive for a top journal; with these revisions, it would be a significant contribution.

## Consensus Strengths (all 5 agreed)

1. **The three-level distinction** (discreteness / individuation / countability) is the paper's clearest standalone contribution.
2. **The dissociation hierarchy is a genuine, falsifiable empirical claim.** The triangular pattern and its falsification conditions impressed everyone.
3. **Functional anchoring is original.** The *police*/*officer* stability story was singled out by 4 of 5 reviewers as the most novel contribution.

## Consensus Weaknesses (3+ reviewers)

1. **The precision hierarchy is informal to the point of circularity** (Slater, Rothstein, Goldberg, Reviewer 2). Without a formal derivation from independently motivated semantics (Rothstein's counting/measuring, Grimm's atomic accessibility, Hackl's degree semantics), the ordering is read off the data it claims to explain.

2. **Bidirectional inference is underdetermined and does too much work** (Slater, Rothstein, Goldberg, Reviewer 2). It explains acquisition, processing, production, diachronic change, and construction-sensitivity — all from one unfalsifiably flexible mechanism with no confirmed independent evidence.

3. **The empirical base is too narrow** (Slater, Rothstein, Reviewer 2). Seven nouns, four in the corpus study, very small counts for quasi-count items. No systematic search for counterexamples.

4. **The *folks* probe is unrun** (Goldberg, Slater, Reviewer 2). The paper's strongest prediction (construction-sensitivity) has no data behind it.

## Issues Raised by 1–2 Reviewers

5. **Projectibility is buried** (Projectibility Reviewer). The conclusion, intro, and alternatives section are mechanism-first. The paper passes the projectibility test in §3.2 but doesn't foreground it as the epistemic payoff.

6. **Underengagement with construction grammar** (Goldberg). "Bidirectional inference" is substantially what CxG calls form-meaning pairing, entrenchment, and statistical preemption.

7. **What would fail the HPC test?** (Reviewer 2). If bidirectional inference applies to any form-meaning cluster, the framework doesn't individuate countability.

## Key Contradiction

**Rothstein on *many*:** Argues *many cattle* is a measure predicate (no individuation needed), not evidence of partial individuation. If correct, the "loose" end of the hierarchy isn't part of the count cluster at all, and the implicational structure collapses to binary count/not-count. Most dangerous objection.

## Prioritized Revision List (Top 5)

1. **Formalize precision** — commit to counting vs. measuring (Rothstein) or atomic accessibility (Grimm) enough to derive tight/loose ordering independently. Address the *many*-as-measure objection head-on.

2. **Promote projectibility** — restructure intro to frame puzzle as "why does knowing one property predict others?" and conclusion to cash out what HPC status *buys*.

3. **Engage construction grammar explicitly** — acknowledge the core mechanism isn't new; identify what HPC framing adds (implicational ordering, perturbation-sensitivity, functional anchoring).

4. **Broaden the empirical base** — add *poultry*, *vermin*, *livestock*, *clergy*, *staff*, *youth* to matrix. Search for counterexamples.

5. **Resolve the unrun probe** — run it or compress §5.3 to a paragraph.
